### 🩺 What I'm building now

**[VisitRecall](https://visitrecall.com)** — an ambient AI scribe for patients. The *patient's* scribe, not the doctor's.

You record a doctor's visit on your phone, and VisitRecall transcribes it, explains the medical terms in plain English, pulls out your action items and follow-ups, decodes the bill, and tracks your medications, diagnoses, vitals, and labs across providers over time. It also runs AI voice and SMS agents that book follow-up appointments and chase down referrals on your behalf.

### 🤝 What I think about a lot

I've become a little obsessed with how AI tools are collapsing the distance between **product managers, designers, and engineers**.

To be clear about what I *don't* mean: I don't think this is the end of specialists, and I'm not an evangelist for solo building. The opposite, actually. Great products still need a **team** — and the best teams are built from people who go genuinely deep in one craft, not a roster of generalists. What's changed is that when everyone at the table can *understand*, and even *do a bit of*, each other's jobs, the whole team gets sharper and the product gets better. Deep specialists with wide empathy beat generalists every time.

### 🧪 How I build: make the discipline a property of the system

"Follow Lean Startup" is a guideline, and a guideline is willpower — willpower is the thing that fails under shipping pressure. So I turned it into a **CI gate**: a net-new feature can't merge until the bet behind it is written down and *falsifiable* — a numeric success threshold, a review-by date, a pre-committed **persevere / pivot / kill** rule, and a metric wired to real code. The kill line is the one founders skip, so it's the one the gate forces. The gate checks one thing: that the bet is on record and well-formed — it doesn't judge whether the hypothesis is *good*. That's my job.
